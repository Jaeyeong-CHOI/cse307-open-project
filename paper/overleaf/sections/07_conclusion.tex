\section{Conclusion}
We presented a four-level taxonomy (L1--L4) for designing LLM-resistant programming languages, grounded in the NTP prior structure of language models.

Our two strongest findings are: (1)~\emph{L4 pattern blindness defeats all tested frontier models}---implicit semantic inversion on prior-entrenched tasks yields 0/350 pass across eleven models including \texttt{gpt-5.4} and \texttt{o3}; and (2)~\emph{chain-of-thought does not help}---models articulate the correct inversion rule yet still generate standard Python, confirming a generative failure rather than a comprehension deficit.

I/O opacity provides a practical second resistance pathway: opaque input--output pairs alone yield complete resistance (0/30) independent of prior strength, and can be combined with L4 semantic inversion for layered assessment design.

We identify three distinct failure mechanisms---\emph{prior dominance}, \emph{pattern blindness}, and \emph{operational substitution}---each with different implications for language design. These findings provide a principled basis for designing programming assessments that remain meaningful in the LLM era.
