\subsection{L1: Partial resistance; scale does not predict compliance}
All models show non-zero KLR (0.21--0.69), confirming prior dominance. Three findings stand out: (1) gpt-5.4-mini (KLR=0.67) underperforms gpt-4o (KLR=0.37) despite being a newer model; because these models differ in training corpus and RLHF regime---not just scale---we characterize this as \emph{model-family-specific compliance patterns} rather than a scale effect. The observed difference may reflect stronger code-generation fine-tuning in gpt-5.4-mini (a stated hypothesis, not a measured mechanism); (2) for models with paired conditions, context-pack delivery reduces KLR: gpt-4o-mini ($0.42 \to 0.26$, $-38\%$) and gpt-4.1-mini ($0.43 \to 0.21$, $-51\%$); (3) the reasoning model o4-mini (KLR=0.27) performs comparably to gpt-4o-mini ctx-pack (0.26), suggesting chain-of-thought reasoning provides modest benefit for alias compliance. Note: gpt-4.1, gpt-4.1-nano, and o4-mini were only evaluated under ctx-pack ($n=20$, v1--v20); baseline comparison for these models is not available and should be interpreted with caution given the smaller and non-overlapping prompt subset.

\subsection{L2: Syntax inversion not resistant}
All models adopted L2 syntax fully (SIR=0.0) when given explicit rules. Structural novelty alone does not create LLM resistance.

\subsection{L3: Capability threshold at semantic rule-following}
With an explicit semantic inversion rule, gpt-4o-mini fails completely (PPR=1.0): it generates standard Python despite instruction. gpt-4o and gpt-4.1-mini succeed (PPR=0.0). This reveals a model capacity threshold for following semantic meta-rules.

\subsection{L4: Pattern blindness across all tested models}
\label{sec:l4_ablation}
Without explicit rule, gpt-4o---which succeeds at L3---fails at L4 (PPR=1.0), producing canonical Python and ignoring example-embedded inversion cues. Figure~\ref{fig:l4_failure} shows the contrast.

\textbf{L4 ablation (n=50 per model).}
We scaled the L4 evaluation across seven models, all at $n=50$ (gpt-4o, gpt-4o-mini, gpt-4.1, gpt-4.1-mini, gpt-4.1-nano, gpt-5.4-mini, o4-mini).
Results are shown in Table~\ref{tab:l4_ablation}.
All seven models achieve 0\% pass rate (0/50 each), confirming that pattern blindness is robust across model families and including the reasoning model o4-mini.
PPR values range from 0.38 (gpt-4.1-mini) to 1.0 (gpt-4.1, gpt-4.1-nano, gpt-5.4-mini); o4-mini shows PPR=0.92.
Notably, gpt-4.1 and gpt-4.1-nano show PPR=1.0 across \emph{all} 50 runs and all variants, indicating complete prior dominance with no evidence of inversion detection.
The o4-mini result revises an earlier preliminary finding (1/20 at $n=20$): at the full scale of $n=50$, o4-mini also achieves 0/50, indicating that chain-of-thought reasoning does not provide a reliable pathway to overcoming pattern blindness at this scale.

\subsection{L4 Multi-task Generalization}
To assess whether pattern blindness extends beyond Fibonacci, we evaluated all seven models on three tasks: T1 (\texttt{fibonacci}), T2 (\texttt{is\_sorted}), and T3 (\texttt{count\_vowels}). The multi-task prompt uses inline code comments that partially annotate the inversion rule (\textit{e.g.}, \texttt{\# runs when a > b is FALSE, i.e., a <= b}); this is a \emph{partially-annotated L4} condition---less explicit than L3 (no stated rule), but more informative than the pure example-only variants used in the ablation. \texttt{gpt-4o}, \texttt{gpt-4o-mini}, \texttt{gpt-5.4-mini}, and \texttt{o4-mini} were evaluated at $n=10$ or $n=15$ per task; \texttt{gpt-4.1-mini} at $n=20$ per task; \texttt{gpt-4.1} and \texttt{gpt-4.1-nano} at $n=10$ per task (255 initial runs). We further scaled gpt-4o T3 and gpt-4.1 T2 to $n=50$ each to resolve preliminary ambiguities, yielding 355 total multi-task runs. Results are in Table~\ref{tab:l4_multitask}.

Seven findings emerge. (1) \textbf{T2 is extractable only by mid-to-large models}: gpt-4o (10/10) and gpt-4.1-mini (20/20) pass T2 fully, while gpt-4.1 (1/50, PPR=0.42), gpt-4.1-nano (0/10), gpt-5.4-mini (0/10), and gpt-4o-mini (0/10) fail. This reveals a non-monotonic capacity threshold: T2 extractability does not scale linearly within or across model families. (2) \textbf{T1 (Fibonacci) is resistant for all non-reasoning models}: six of seven models fail T1, with high or full PPR (PPR=1.0 for gpt-4o, gpt-4.1, gpt-4.1-mini, gpt-5.4-mini; PPR=0.2 for gpt-4.1-nano; template fragmentation for gpt-4o-mini). The exception is o4-mini, which achieves 8/15 under the partially-annotated prompt (after correcting a token budget truncation; see $^\S$)---consistent with reasoning-model capability to leverage partial annotation cues that dense models overlook. (3) \textbf{Operational substitution dominates T3}: the scale-up (gpt-4o T3, $n=50$) reveals operational substitution in 47/50 runs (94\%; Wilson 95\% CI: [83\%, 99\%])---the model applies arithmetic transformations (\texttt{count -= 1; return -count}) to achieve correct output without adopting the L4 semantic rule. The preliminary $n=10$ result (1 pass) was a statistical artifact; at $n=50$, gpt-4o achieves 0/50 pass (Wilson 95\% CI: [0\%, 7.1\%]). gpt-5.4-mini T3 also exhibits operational substitution (PPR=0.0, all 10 runs). This confirms operational substitution as a structurally distinct, frequent failure mode in capable models. (4) \textbf{Model capacity determines T2 extractability}: gpt-4o-mini, gpt-4.1, gpt-4.1-nano, and gpt-5.4-mini all fail T2, while gpt-4o and gpt-4.1-mini succeed---consistent with the capacity threshold observed at L3. (5) \textbf{Reasoning partially mitigates pattern blindness under partial annotation}: o4-mini achieves 8/15 on T1 under the partially-annotated prompt (after token budget correction), but fails T2 and T3 (0/15 each; overall 8/45). Under the strict example-only ablation (§~\ref{sec:l4_ablation}), o4-mini achieves 0/50---chain-of-thought reasoning alone cannot overcome pure pattern blindness, but reasoning models can exploit partial annotation cues that dense models miss. (6) \textbf{Within-family inconsistency is confirmed}: gpt-4.1 T2 scale-up ($n=50$) confirms the capacity gap is systematic---1/50 pass (Wilson 95\% CI: [0\%, 10.5\%]) vs.\ gpt-4.1-mini 20/20 (Fisher's exact $p < 0.0001$). Fine-tuning or RLHF choices appear to play a larger role than raw scale in determining L4 pattern extraction capability. (7) \textbf{gpt-5.4-mini shows uniform prior dominance across all tasks}: 0/30 with T1 PPR=1.0, T2 PPR=1.0, and T3 operational substitution pattern, consistent with its L4 ablation PPR=1.0 on Fibonacci variants.

\subsection{Three Failure Modes}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Prior Dominance} (L1, L3-weak, L4-T2-mini, L4-T1-all): stated or implicit rule is overridden by pretrained token distribution.
  \item \textbf{Pattern Blindness} (L4-T1): model cannot extract the semantic rule from examples alone, even when the same model follows the rule when stated explicitly (L3). T1 (Fibonacci) shows universal pattern blindness across all models.
  \item \textbf{Operational Substitution} (L4-T3-gpt-4o, $n=50$): model compensates via arithmetic output transformation rather than adopting the semantic inversion rule, indicating goal awareness without rule internalization. Confirmed as dominant at 94\% of T3 gpt-4o runs (Wilson 95\% CI: [83\%, 99\%]).
\end{itemize}
We also observe \emph{template fragmentation} in gpt-4o-mini T1 (split base cases, non-standard recursion template): this is a variant of prior dominance in which the model partially disrupts its standard template but does not achieve semantic inversion, yielding an incorrect but structurally novel output. We treat this as a sub-variant of prior dominance rather than a distinct fourth mode, given the small sample (n=10); further study at larger n may warrant reclassification.
Task difficulty interacts with model capacity to determine which failure mode dominates. T2 (is\_sorted) is extractable by mid-to-large models; T1 (Fibonacci) is universally resistant; T3 (count\_vowels) triggers operational substitution as the dominant escape strategy for capable models.
