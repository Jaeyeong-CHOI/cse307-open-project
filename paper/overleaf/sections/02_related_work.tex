\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Code Generation Benchmarks and Evaluation}
HumanEval~\cite{chen2021codex} and MBPP~\cite{austin2021program} established functional correctness as the primary metric for code-generation evaluation. More recent work scales and diversifies these benchmarks: BigCodeBench~\cite{bigcodebench2024} adds diverse function calls and complex instructions, while LiveCodeBench~\cite{livecodebench2024} addresses contamination by continuously updating with new problems. Surveys by Zan et al.~\cite{zan2024codesurvey} and Liu et al.~\cite{liu2024codeeval} document rapid progress on these benchmarks while noting limitations: performance on real-world class-level code generation (24--35\%) lags far behind synthetic benchmarks (84--89\%), motivating evaluation beyond functional correctness.

\subsection{Code-Oriented LLM Architectures and Training}
Pre-trained code models including CodeBERT~\cite{feng2020codebert}, CodeT5~\cite{wang2021codet5}, and InCoder~\cite{fried2022incoder} demonstrated that encoder-decoder and fill-in-middle objectives reinforce Python token distribution priors. More recent instruction-tuned models (WizardCoder~\cite{luo2023wizardcoder}, DeepSeek-Coder~\cite{guo2024deepseek}) amplify these priors further through specialized code pretraining. This prior-amplification trend directly motivates our L1 findings: stronger priors lead to higher keyword leakage.

\subsection{Instruction-Following in Code Generation (2024--2025)}
CodeIF~\cite{codeif2025} and MultiCodeIF~\cite{multicode2025} introduce benchmarks specifically measuring LLM adherence to explicit developer-specified constraints. Both find a gap between general programming competence and instruction compliance. Instructional robustness research~\cite{instructrobust2025,instructgap2025} shows that even state-of-the-art models fail to reliably follow nuanced instructions in enterprise contexts. Our study extends this line to an extreme case: instructions that semantically invert Python priors.

\subsection{Instruction Hierarchy and Prior Conflicts}
Chen et al.~\cite{chen2024instructhierarchy} propose an \emph{instruction hierarchy} to teach models to prioritize system-level instructions over user instructions, addressing a primary alignment vulnerability. Their setting involves natural-language directive conflicts; ours involves programming-language semantic conflicts, which remain underexplored. Xie et al.~\cite{xie2024conflict} and Wu et al.~\cite{wu2024contextparametric} study the broader problem of parametric vs.\ contextual knowledge, showing LLMs often default to pretraining priors under conflict. Zhang et al.~\cite{zhang2024conflict} survey knowledge conflicts comprehensively but focus on factual knowledge rather than code semantics. Shi et al.~\cite{contextinvert2024} show LLMs are distracted by irrelevant context; our L4 condition represents the dual phenomenon: LLMs \emph{ignore} relevant semantic-inversion cues in examples.

\subsection{NTP Priors and In-Context Learning}
Wang et al.~\cite{wang2024ntp} formalize a ``law of next-token prediction'' showing each layer contributes equally to prior-driven prediction, providing theoretical grounding for our observation that token predictability drives prior dominance. Min et al.~\cite{min2022rethinking} show that ICL demonstrations matter primarily through surface patterns rather than label content. Dong et al.~\cite{dong2022survey} survey ICL systematically; and Lampinen et al.~\cite{anotb2024} document ``A-Not-B'' errors where LLMs fail to suppress previously learned response patterns under context shifts, directly analogous to our pattern blindness finding.

\subsection{Counterfactual and Semantic Reasoning Failures}
Zhao et al.~\cite{counterfact2024} show LLMs struggle with counterfactual reasoning that requires overriding parametric knowledge. Turpin et al.~\cite{semanticinvar2024} demonstrate LLM self-explanations are often unfaithful under semantic framing changes. Valmeekam et al.~\cite{reasoningrigidity2024} document reasoning rigidity---models revert to memorized strategies when game parameters are redefined---which we observe as pattern blindness in the L4 condition.

\subsection{Adversarial Perturbation and LLM-Resistant Design}
PromptBench~\cite{zhu2023promptrobust} shows LLMs are fragile under adversarial text perturbations. Pan et al.~\cite{pan2024adversarial} show semantically aligned code prompt attacks cause up to 42\% accuracy drops. Jha et al.~\cite{coderobbery2024} introduce ``break-the-chain'' attacks on LLM code generation through structured prompt perturbations. Khan et al.~\cite{khan2024cheatingllm} design adversarial perturbations specifically to reduce LLM correctness on programming assignments, achieving a 77\% correctness reduction. Our approach differs: rather than perturbing prompts, we design a principled confusion language whose NTP prior conflict is a structural property.

\subsection{Domain-Specific Language (DSL) Evaluation}
Wu et al.~\cite{wu2024lowresource} survey LLM performance on low-resource and DSLs, finding models trained on Python perform substantially worse on less common languages. ConCodeEval~\cite{concode2024} benchmarks code constraint comprehension within DSLs, revealing that general code proficiency does not transfer to constraint-following. L2CEval~\cite{l2ceval2024} evaluates language-to-code generation across semantic parsing and specialized languages. Constrained decoding approaches~\cite{dong2024xgrammar,muendler2025type} show that grammar enforcement can improve output validity; our L2 experiment shows models adopt new syntax easily when given explicit rules, suggesting grammar-level constraints are insufficient without semantic constraints.

\subsection{Real-World Software Engineering Benchmarks}
SWE-bench~\cite{jimenez2024swebench} and SWE-agent~\cite{yang2024swebench2} reveal the gap between benchmark performance and real repository-level tasks. These works confirm that high pass rates on canonical benchmarks do not generalize to structural novelty, supporting our hypothesis that Python-trained LLMs collapse under structurally inverted semantics.

\subsection{LLM in Programming Education}
Dakhel et al.~\cite{dakhel2023github} show GitHub Copilot is an ``asset or liability'' depending on student expertise. Lau and Guo~\cite{lau2023learners} observe novice programmers adopting LLM outputs uncritically. Prather et al.~\cite{prather2023robots} document mixed perceptions of AI assistance among students. Wermelinger~\cite{wermelinger2023copilot} benchmarks Copilot on simple exercises. Liffiton et al.~\cite{liffiton2023codehelp} propose guardrailed LLM tutoring systems. Collectively, these studies motivate the need for assessment mechanisms that preserve student skill development.

\subsection{Academic Integrity and LLM-Resistant Assessment}
The impact of LLMs on academic integrity is empirically documented across multiple studies. Novak and McDanel~\cite{novak2025llmhw} propose contextual and personalized assignments; Shoufan~\cite{aiintegrity2024} studies student perceptions of ChatGPT; Llobet Sanch\'is et al.~\cite{llobet2025study} conduct a scoping review of AI integrity impacts. Detection-based approaches (code perplexity~\cite{codeplagiarism2024}) are brittle; our taxonomy offers a complementary design-based solution.

\subsection{LLM Behavior, Evaluation, and Reasoning}
Perez et al.~\cite{sycophancy2023} discover sycophantic behavior in LLMs; Wang et al.~\cite{wang2023selfconsistency} propose self-consistency decoding. Ganguli et al.~\cite{ganguli2022red} study red-teaming methods for eliciting harmful behavior, a setting related to probing model boundaries. Liang et al.~\cite{liang2023helm}, Chang et al.~\cite{chang2024survey}, and Srivastava et al.~\cite{srivastava2023bigbench} provide holistic evaluation frameworks. Wei et al.~\cite{wei2022cot} and Kojima et al.~\cite{kojima2022zerocot} show chain-of-thought prompting improves multi-step reasoning; however, Turpin et al.~\cite{semanticinvar2024} show CoT explanations are often unfaithful, cautioning against treating CoT success as evidence of genuine semantic understanding.

\subsection{Summary and Research Gap}
Collectively, prior work shows: (1) LLMs have strong Python token priors; (2) instruction-following degrades under conflict with parametric knowledge; (3) adversarial attacks degrade code quality but without principled design theory; (4) educational assessments are being undermined by LLMs. Our contribution unifies these threads: a four-level NTP-prior-grounded taxonomy (L1--L4) that explains \emph{why} specific language features produce specific failure modes, enabling principled design of LLM-resistant educational assessments.
